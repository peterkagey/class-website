### Choice Exercise 5 [3]
In complex analysis, we define $i$ as the "imaginary unit" so that $i^2 = -1$, and thus $i^3 = -i$, and so on.

1. We know that $$
    i^0 = 1, \qquad i^1 = i, \qquad i^2 = -1, \quad \text{and} \quad i^3 = -i.
  $$
  Prove (by repeated multiplication) that $$
    i^4 = 1, \qquad i^5 = i, \qquad i^6 = -1, \quad \text{and} \quad i^7 = -i.
  $$
2. Prove that if $n$ and $m$ are positive integers and $n \equiv m \pmod 4$, then $i^n = i^m$. <br> *(Hint: choose $n \geq m$. Why can you do this?)*
3. Compute $i^{919}$.

### Choice Exercise 7 [≤7]
*Do any subset of the following, up to a maximum of seven points.*

Prove the following divisibility tricks that you may have learned back when you were first learning arithmetic.
1. **[1]** Many of these tricks depend on the facts like $10^n \equiv 1 \pmod 9$ for all integers $n \ge 0$.
2. **[2]** Let $n$ be an integer and let $s_n$ be the sum of its digits. For example, if $n = 7261992$ then $s_n = 7+2+6+1+9+9+2=36$, and note that
  $$
    7261992 = 7 \times 10^6 + 2 \times 10^5 + \dots + 9 \times 10^1 + 2 \times 10^0.
  $$
  Prove that if $3 \mid s_n$, then $3 \mid n$.
1. **[2]** Prove the other direction: if $3 \mid n$ then $3 \mid s_n$.
2. **[2]** Let $a_n$ be the *alternating* sum of the digits of $n$. For example, if $n = 7261992$ then $$a_n = 7-2+6-1+9-9+2=12.$$ Prove that if $11 \mid a_n$ then $11 \mid n$.
3. **[2]** Prove that if $11 \mid n$ then $11 \mid a_n$.
4. **[3]** Let $d_n$ be the number given by the last two digits of $n$. For example, if $n = 7261992$ then $d_n=92$.<br>Prove that if $4 \mid n$ then $4 \mid d_n$ *and* prove that if $4 \mid d_n$ then $4 \mid n$
5. **[3]** Let $T_n$ be the number given by "tripling" $n$, so if $m = 19$ then $T_m=191919$.<br>Prove that if the number of digits in $n$ is NOT a multiple of $3$, then $37 \mid T_n$.
6. **[3]** Prove that if the number of digits in $n$ is a multiple of $3$, then $37 \nmid T_n$.
7. **[4]** Take the last digit of $n$, double it, and subtract it from the initial digits. Prove that if this is divisible by $7$, then $n$ is divisible by $7$. (For example, if $n = 259$, you would do $25-18=7$, and thus $7 \mid n$.)
