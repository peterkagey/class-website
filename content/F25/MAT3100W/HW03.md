## Required Exercises.
*Do all 10 points of the following required exercises.*

### Required Exercise [1 pt]
We're doing a lot of modular arithmetic, you should know how to typeset it.

  1. Type `\(726 \equiv 23 \pmod{19}\)` to get $726 \equiv 23 \pmod{19}$.
  2. Prove or disprove that $726 \equiv 23 \pmod{19}$.

### Required Exercise 2 [3 pts]
Suppose $a \equiv a' \pmod m$ and $b \equiv b' \pmod m$. Prove the following:
  1. $a + b \equiv a' + b' \pmod m$.
  2. $a - b \equiv a' - b' \pmod m$.
  3. $a \times b \equiv a' \times b' \pmod m$.

### Required Exercise 3 [3 pts]
  1. Prove that $10^n \equiv 1 \pmod 3$ for all integers $n > 0$.
  2. Let $s(n)$ be the sum of the digits of an integer $n>0$. For example,
    $$
      s(7261992)=7+2+6+1+9+9+2=36.
    $$
    Prove that if $3 \mid s(n)$, then $3 \mid n$.

  (Note that $7269 = 7 \times 10^3 + 2 \times 10^2 + 6 \times 10^1 + 7 \times 10^0$.)

### Choice Exercise 6 [3 pts]
In complex analysis, we define $i$ as the "imaginary unit" so that $i^2 = -1$, and thus $i^3 = -i$, and so on.

1. We know that $$
    i^0 = 1, \qquad i^1 = i, \qquad i^2 = -1, \quad \text{and} \quad i^3 = -i.
  $$
  Prove (by repeated multiplication) that $$
    i^4 = 1, \qquad i^5 = i, \qquad i^6 = -1, \quad \text{and} \quad i^7 = -i.
  $$
2. Prove that if $n$ and $m$ are positive integers and $n \equiv m \pmod 4$, then $i^n = i^m$. <br> *(Hint: choose $n \geq m$. Why can you do this?)*
3. Compute $i^{919}$.

### Choice Exercise 8 [≤6 points]
*Do any subset of the following, up to a maximum of six points.*

Let $n$ be a positive integer, let$s(n)$ be the digit sum of $n$, and let $a(n)$ be the alternating sum of $n$. For example, $$
    \begin{align}
      s(7261992)&=7+2+6+1+9+9+2=36 \\\\
      a(7261992)&=7-2+6-1+9-9+2=12.
    \end{align}
  $$
1. **[2 pts]** Prove that if $3 \mid n$ then $3 \mid s(n)$.
2. **[2 pts]** Prove that if $11 \mid a(n)$ then $11 \mid n$.
3. **[2 pts]** Prove that if $11 \mid n$ then $11 \mid a(n)$.
4. **[2 pts]** Let $d(n)$ be the number given by the last two digits of $n$, so that $a(7261992)=92$. Prove that if $4 \mid n$ then $4 \mid d(n)$.
5. **[3 pts]** Let $T(n)$ be the number given by "tripling" $n$, so that $a(19)=191919$. Prove that if the number of digits in $n$ is NOT a multiple of $3$, then $37 \mid T(n)$.
6. **[3 pts]** Prove that if the number of digits in $n$ is a multiple of $3$, then $37 \nmid T(n)$.
7. **[4 pts]** Take the last digit of $n$, double it, and subtract it from the initial digits. Prove that if this is divisible by $7$, then $n$ is divisible by $7$. (For example, if $n = 259$, you would do $25-18=7$, and thus $7 \mid n$.)
