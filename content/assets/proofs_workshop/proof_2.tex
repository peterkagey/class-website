Prove that if all the coefficients of the quadratic equation:

\[ax^2 + bx + c = 0\]

\noindent
are odd integers, then the roots of the equation cannot be rational.

\begin{proof}

Deriving the quadratic equation to solve x, we get:

\[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{a^2}\]

\noindent
By definition, in order to get a rational number, \(\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}\) must be a perfect square. If all numbers are odd, then we know that \(4ac\) can be written as:

\[4(2n+1)(2m+1) = 4(4nm+2n+2m+1) = 16nm+8n+8m+4, \text{ where n and m are integers.}\]

\noindent
We can write this in modular arithmetic as:

\[4ac \equiv 4 \pmod8.\]

\noindent
Now for the other part of the square root, \(b^2\). We know that whenever there is an odd square, it is congruent to \(1 \pmod8\).

\noindent
Adding both of these components up, we get

\[b^2 - 4ac \equiv 1 - 4 \pmod8 = -3 \equiv 5 \pmod8.\]

\noindent
Remember, a perfect square can be written in either form

\[k = 0 \pmod8 \text{ or } k = 4\pmod8.\]

\noindent
Since we ended with the form \(k = 5 \pmod8\), we know that \(b^2 - 4ac\) can never be a perfect square, and makes the square root irrational. Therefore, whenever all coefficients of the quadratic equation are odd, the roots of the equation cannot be rational.
\end{proof}
